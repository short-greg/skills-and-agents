# Communication Style

Techniques for calibrating how to communicate — adjusting directness, formality, verbosity, and tone.

Match communication style dimensions to context and audience.

## Goal

Enable effective communication by matching style dimensions to context and audience.

---

## Intent

How you communicate affects reception as much as what you communicate. A well-chosen message with mismatched style falls flat. Directness, formality, verbosity, technical level, and tone must align with situation and audience for communication to land effectively.

---

## Scope

Techniques for adjusting communication style dimensions: directness (direct vs indirect), formality (formal vs casual), verbosity (concise vs detailed), technical level (jargon vs plain language), tone (professional, friendly, empathetic), interaction framing (defining what we're doing), and stance (how to engage). Applies to any communication where style affects reception.

## Core Approaches

### Directness Calibration

Use directness techniques to match communication force to situation requirements.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Direct Communication** | High stakes, clear authority, explicit request for directness, or low-context cultures | To communicate unambiguously and efficiently | Use imperative mood, state conclusion first, eliminate hedging words, be explicit about expectations | Styled Message | Is meaning unambiguous? Is action clear? | Could the statement be misunderstood? Is it softer than intended? |
| **Indirect Communication** | Relationship preservation matters, hierarchy is sensitive, face-saving needed, or high-context cultures | To influence without commanding | Use question form ("Have you considered...?"), present as observation, offer rather than instruct, use conditional language | Styled Message | Is suggestion clear despite indirection? Is face preserved? | Is it so indirect the point is lost? Does it seem passive-aggressive? |
| **Graduated Directness** | Recipient's receptiveness is unknown | To calibrate force based on response | Start with softest form that could succeed, observe reception, increase directness if needed, match escalation to resistance | — | Is escalation path planned? Are you reading reception signals? | Are you starting too soft or too strong? |
| **Context-Matched Force** | Different audiences or situations require different force | To match directness to context norms | Assess relationship (peer, subordinate, superior), assess culture (direct vs indirect), assess stakes (high vs low), adjust force accordingly | Styled Message | Does force match context? Would audience expect this level? | Is directness mismatched to relationship or culture? |

### Formality Selection

Use formality techniques to match tone to relationship and setting.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Formal Style** | Professional settings, first contact, hierarchical relationships, or sensitive topics | To convey professionalism and respect | Use complete sentences, avoid contractions, use formal vocabulary, follow conventions, maintain distance | Styled Message | Is language appropriately formal? Is respect conveyed? | Is formality excessive or stuffy? |
| **Semi-Formal Style** | Established professional relationships, collaborative work, or technical discussions | To balance professionalism with approachability | Use mostly complete sentences, occasional contractions acceptable, mix formal and casual vocabulary, maintain professional tone | Styled Message | Is balance between formal and casual appropriate? | Is style inconsistent or unclear? |
| **Casual Style** | Peer relationships, informal settings, or creative brainstorming | To build rapport and encourage openness | Use contractions freely, conversational vocabulary, shorter sentences, allow personality | Styled Message | Is casualness appropriate to context? Does it build rapport? | Is casualness unprofessional or disrespectful? |

### Verbosity Calibration

Use verbosity techniques to match detail level to audience needs.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Concise Communication** | Time-constrained audiences, executives, or when clarity demands brevity | To deliver key messages efficiently | Eliminate unnecessary words, provide only essential information, use active voice, lead with conclusion | Styled Message | Is message clear with minimum words? Are key points prominent? | Is brevity sacrificing clarity or important context? |
| **Moderate Detail** | General audiences, balanced explanations, or standard documentation | To provide adequate context without overwhelming | Include key details and context, explain non-obvious points, balance brevity with completeness | Styled Message | Is detail level appropriate? Is context sufficient? | Is detail unbalanced (too much in some areas, too little in others)? |
| **Detailed Communication** | Technical audiences needing depth, documentation, or complex explanations | To provide comprehensive information | Include background, rationale, examples, edge cases, caveats, explain reasoning thoroughly | Styled Message | Is depth appropriate to audience needs? Is reasoning clear? | Is detail overwhelming or obscuring key points? |

### Technical Level Adjustment

Use technical level techniques to match language to audience expertise.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Technical Language** | Expert audiences, same-domain communication, or precision required | To communicate efficiently with shared vocabulary | Use domain jargon freely, assume background knowledge, reference technical concepts directly, prioritize precision | Styled Message | Is jargon appropriate to audience? Is precision maintained? | Is language excluding those who should understand? |
| **Plain Language** | Non-technical audiences, cross-domain communication, or public-facing content | To ensure understanding across expertise levels | Avoid jargon, define necessary technical terms, use analogies, prioritize clarity over precision | Styled Message | Is message understandable to target audience? Are concepts clear? | Is oversimplification losing important nuance? |
| **Adaptive Technical Level** | Mixed audiences with varying expertise | To serve multiple expertise levels simultaneously | Provide high-level summary first, layer in technical details, define terms on first use, offer examples for clarity | Styled Message | Can both experts and non-experts follow? Is layering clear? | Is one group lost or bored? |

### Tone Selection

Use tone techniques to match emotional tenor to situation.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Professional Tone** | Business contexts, formal settings, or neutral stance needed | To maintain appropriate boundaries and objectivity | Use neutral language, avoid emotional language, focus on facts and outcomes, maintain consistent voice | Styled Message | Is tone appropriately professional? Is objectivity maintained? | Is tone cold or dismissive? |
| **Friendly Tone** | Building rapport, peer interactions, or collaborative work | To create approachable atmosphere | Use warm language, show interest, acknowledge contributions, express appreciation when appropriate | Styled Message | Does tone build rapport? Is warmth genuine? | Is friendliness inappropriate or distracting? |
| **Empathetic Tone** | Sensitive situations, feedback delivery, or emotional contexts | To acknowledge feelings while maintaining purpose | Acknowledge difficulty or emotions, show understanding, validate concerns, maintain supportive stance | Styled Message | Is empathy genuine and appropriate? Is purpose maintained? | Is empathy performative or undermining the message? |
| **Assertive Tone** | Clear boundaries needed, decisions required, or authority appropriate | To communicate confidence and conviction | State positions clearly, use confident language, avoid unnecessary hedging, maintain respect | Styled Message | Is assertion appropriate and respectful? Is conviction clear? | Is assertion aggressive or dismissive? |

### Interaction Framing

Use framing techniques to establish what kind of interaction this is.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Activity Typing** | Interaction type affects appropriate behavior | To set expectations for what will happen | Name the activity (debugging, designing, reviewing, brainstorming, implementing), identify activity-appropriate behaviors, exclude inappropriate ones | Interaction Frame | Is activity type explicit? Do behaviors match? | Are you debugging when you should be designing? |
| **Goal Framing** | Purpose of interaction needs clarity | To align effort with intended outcome | State what we're trying to achieve, distinguish exploration from execution, identify success conditions for the interaction | Interaction Frame | Is goal explicit? Is exploration vs execution clear? | Is purpose unclear or shifting? |
| **Constraint Framing** | Constraints shape valid options | To work within bounds productively | List constraints (time, resources, compatibility, scope), accept as given not obstacles, find solutions within bounds | Interaction Frame | Are constraints explicit and accepted? | Are you fighting constraints rather than accepting? |
| **Scope Framing** | Work boundaries need definition | To focus effort appropriately | Define what's in scope, define what's explicitly out, resist scope creep by referencing frame | Interaction Frame | Are boundaries explicit? Is scope creep prevented? | Is scope undefined or expanding? |

### Stance Setting

Use stance techniques to establish how you will engage in the interaction.

| Technique | When | Why | How | Output/Artifact | Positive Validation | Negative Validation |
|-----------|------|-----|-----|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Footing Selection** | Relationship to other party matters | To adopt appropriate relational position | Choose footing: authoritative (I know, you follow), collaborative (we figure out together), advisory (I suggest, you decide), instructive (I teach, you learn) | Stance Specification | Is footing explicit and appropriate? | Is footing mismatched to situation? |
| **Mode Selection** | Engagement style matters | To match interaction dynamics | Choose mode: direct (conclusions first), exploratory (questions first), systematic (step-by-step), creative (divergent), critical (evaluative) | Stance Specification | Is mode explicit and maintained? | Are you defaulting to habitual mode? |
| **Engagement Calibration** | Energy level affects reception | To match interaction energy to context | Assess appropriate energy (enthusiastic, measured, serious), match language intensity to energy level, maintain consistency | Stance Specification | Is engagement level appropriate? Is energy consistent? | Is energy mismatched (too high/low for context)? |

## Possible Actions

Select or execute possible actions based on context; this list is not exhaustive. Actions describe what to do; use techniques from Core Approaches for how.

| Action | Goal | When | How |
|--------|------|------|-----|
| **Assess Style Context** | To determine appropriate style dimensions for context and recipient | Use when starting engagement or context has changed | Analyze task domain, stakes, audience; infer or ask about recipient preferences; identify which dimensions need adjustment |
| **Calibrate Style** | To set communication parameters for task and user | Use when style not yet configured or needs reset | Set directness, formality, tone, verbosity, and technical level based on context assessment |
| **Frame Interaction** | To establish what kind of activity this is | Use when interaction type affects appropriate behaviors | Name the activity; state goal explicitly; define constraints and scope |
| **Set Stance** | To establish how you will engage | Use when default engagement mode doesn't fit | Choose footing (authoritative, collaborative, advisory); select mode (direct, exploratory, systematic); calibrate energy |
| **Monitor Style Alignment** | To detect style mismatches during interaction | Use when receiving feedback or sensing friction | Watch for confusion, resistance, or disengagement; identify which dimension is misaligned |
| **Adjust Style** | To shift style dimensions when mismatch detected | Use when monitoring reveals misalignment or context changes | Adjust the mismatched dimension; acknowledge shift if significant; maintain consistency after |

## Risks

These are risks that misuse of this protocol poses to a project with mitigation strategies.

| Risk | When | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|------|---------------------|
| **Directness damage** | Too direct for culture/relationship, or too indirect and meaning lost | Start softer when recipient unknown; read reception signals; escalate directness if message not landing |
| **Formality mismatch** | Too formal feels cold and distant; too casual feels disrespectful | Match relationship and setting; err toward formal for first contact; adjust based on user's tone |
| **Verbosity overwhelm** | Too detailed for time-constrained audience | Lead with conclusion; match detail to demonstrated interest; offer depth optionally |
| **Technical level mismatch** | Jargon alienates non-experts; oversimplification frustrates experts | Assess expertise early; define terms on first use; layer technical detail adaptively |
| **Frame confusion** | Interaction type unclear or wrong activity framed | State frame explicitly when it matters; verify alignment; reframe when activity changes |
| **Compounding misalignment** | Style signals ignored, friction accumulates | Monitor throughout interaction; address small signals early; ask about preferences if uncertain |
| **Inconsistent style** | Shifting style without cause creates confusion | Make deliberate adjustments only; acknowledge significant shifts; maintain consistency within interaction |
